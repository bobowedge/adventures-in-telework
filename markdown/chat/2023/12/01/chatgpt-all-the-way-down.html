ChatGPT All the Way Down

Now, can I let AI do all the work?

Dec 1, 2023 • 9 min read

markdown   chat

In two previous posts (see here and here), I used PyTorch and some tools from the fastai course that I was taking to try to generate some fake chat. The corpus that I used was from a private Google Chat (nee Hangouts) chatroom that I was in. I was trying to generate some chat that at least somewhat resemble the messages that we sent in the room. With mixed result, I’d say.

Of course, this was all done before most people (including myself) were aware of ChatGPT. So, now, naturally, the thing to do is let ChatGPT loose on the input chat and see what it generates. As before, there’s a warning:

Warning: Some of the chat content may contain profanity or stupidity.

For the record, I used ChatGPT version 3.5.

For me, it took a couple of iterations of input into ChatGPT to get it to understand what exactly I wanted and to actually generate some chat. In particular, I learned that there’s a limit on how many input lines from the original chat it could process. I also made sure to give it an idea of the structure of the input data. Here’s the prompt that I ended up using:

Generate the next 50 lines of this chat, where the sender is listed first on each line, followed by ::, followed by the message. Also, images sent have been replaced by the token <MEME>.

Honestly, I’m both impressed and a bit disappointed by this output. I think it does mostly capture the chat, but it accomplishes that by just regurgitating exact lines from the chat. For example, last 10 lines that were output are an exact match to some of the lines of input (complete with the typo ‘Greta’). To be fair, I’m sure we repeat a lot of the same lines (and dub it a ‘callback’), but I think we have more variety in our chats.
